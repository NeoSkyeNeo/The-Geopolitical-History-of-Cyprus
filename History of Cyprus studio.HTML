The Geopolitical History of Cyprus





Location of Cyprus

is the third largest island in the world: located in Asia but considered to be culturally and politically apart of Europe. Cyprus is situated about 80 kilometres (50 miles) south from the west coast of Turkey, 110 kilometres (68 miles) west of Syria, 168 kilometres (104 miles) west of Lebanon, 233 kilometres (145 miles) northwest of Israel, 351 kilometres (218 miles) north of Egypt and 760 kilometres (472 miles) east of Crete, Greece.





A History of External Powers Ruling Cyprus

Cyprus was ruled by: Egypt under Amasis (570–526/525 BCE), The Persian Empire under Cyrus the Great (545 BCE-333 BCE), Alexander the Great (330- June 323 BCE), Alexander's Ptolemy descendant dynasty (323-30 BCE) Roman Empire (30 BCE-330), Abbasid Caliphate (688-965), Byzantine empire (330-1191), House of Lusignan (1194–1205), The Knights Templar (1244-1314), Republic of Venice (1489-1571) and the Ottoman Empire (7th October 1571- 12 June 1878).

Administered sequentially as a British protectorate (1878- 5 November 1914), unilaterally annexed British military occupation (5 November 1914- 10 March 1925) and a Crown colony ( 10 March 1925- 16 August 1960). Currently: there are 2 British SBAs (sovereign base areas) in Cyprus: Akrotiri and Dhekelia





Context of the island- Why was it so valuable?

Due to this island's situation -being at the cross roads of Europe, the Middle East and Northern Africa- it was a great lily pad for outside powers to take control over. The island also had rich natural recourses, including: Gypsum, asbestos, chromite, umber, lime, stone, sand and copper. The copper on the island was mined extensively in ancient times. In fact: it is believed that the name Cyprus is derived from the Greek word meaning copper.





Turks and Greeks Populate the Island

Cyprus had a predominantly Greek population, until the Ottoman Empire conquered the Venetian island of Cyprus on October 7th 1571 in the Battle of Lepanto (a naval engagment by the Holy League). Consequently: many Turks migrated there. In the late 19th century: 3 quarters of Cypriots were ethnically Greek while just a quarter were ethnically Turkish. Yet, for most of Cyprus’ history: they lived harmoniously in a diverse society.






British Cyprus

The Ottoman Empire ceded rule of Cyprus to Britain in July 12th 1878 in exchange for British gaurantee that they would use the country as a base to protect the Ottomans from possible Russian agression. This was in accordance to the Cyprus Convention (secret negotiation between the parties). However, Britain abrogated the agreement on 5 November 1914 when the country declared war on the Ottoman Empire on the same date.

November 5th 1914- May 1st 1925: Cyprus remained a unilaterally annexed military occupation. This annexation was formally recognized by Turkey in July 24th 1923 with the Treaty of Lausanne.

May 1st 1925- August 16th 1960: the island was a crown colony.

The island of Cyprus gained independence from Britain in August 16th 1960.

Today, there are two British Sovereign Base Areas (Akrotiri and Dhekelia) that occupy nearly 3% of the island of Cyprus





1931 Cyprus Revolt - Tension Between Turks and Greeks Grow

The event was known as 1931 Cyprus Revolt or October Events

September 1931. British Governor of Cyprus Ronald Storrs blocked a legislative council decision to halt tax increases that were intended to pay for the budget deficit of £60,000. Greek members of the Legislative Council of the Colony responded by resigning, Bishop of Kitium’s resignation (17th October) triggered the revolt especially. Greek Cypriots were urged to participate in acts of civil disobedience by Archbishop of Kition Nikodemos on 18th October.

October 21- early November, 1931. 5,000 Greek Cypriots (mainly city notables, priests and students) rallied in Nicosia, partaking in chanting pro-Enosis slogans and throwing stones at the house of the British Governor. At this time: people around the country were stripping British flags from public offices and often replaced them with Greek flags. The protestors besieged the government house. The building was burnt down following 3 hours of stone throwing.

The Turk Cypriots, however, wanted Taksim: to partition the island into Greek and Turkish portions. The Cypriots started to define their identities, splitting up based on ethnic and religious loyalties.





London and Zurich Agreements

11 February 1959. An agreement to plan a settlement was reached between Greece and Turkey following a conference in Zurich. The U.K held negotiations in London from 11 February – 19 February. On February 19 1959, the London and Zurich Agreements was signed in Lancaster House, London involving Greek prime minister Konstantinos Karamanlis, Turkish minister of foreign affairs Fatin Rüştü Zorlu, Turkish prime minister Adnan Menderes, the U.K and the Cypriot Community leaders (Archbishop Makarios III for Greek Cypriots and Dr. Fazıl Küçük for Turkish Cypriots). Cyprus accordingly was founded on the same day. The Cypriot constitution was given international standing and written on the basis of the London and Zurich agreement.

Important Implications of the London Zurich Agreement
Cyprus was to become independent

Britain could get to keep both of their Soverign Base Areas and overseas territories in Akrotiri and Dhekelia

The agreements prohibited Cyprus from being a member of any international organization or pacts of alliance that both Turkey and Greece were not a part of. Thus, Enosis and Takism was not allowed.

The President must be a Greek Cypriot (elected by Greek Cypriots) and the Vice president must be a Turkish Cypriot (elected by Turkish Cypriots).

Turkey and Greece would still have some power over Cyprus and could intervene if something goes awry.

Greek and Turkish military forces (at a ratio of 3:2) are to be present in Cyprus at all times and to answer to the Ministers of Turkey, Greece and Cyprus





1974 Cypriot coup d'état

15 July 1974. EOKA-B (a pro-Enosis paramilitary organisation) initiated a Cypriot coup d'état, ordered by the Greek Military Junta. Greece clandestinely aided EOKA-B by providing arms, money and propaganda via radio stations aired from Athens.

The coup d'état’s objectives were to unite Cyprus with Greece (enosis) and to declare the Hellenic Republic of Cyprus. The coup d’état was deemed illegal by the UN (United Nations). The coup triggered a Cypriot invasion by Turkey.

EOKA-B’s campaign had 2 phases: Operation Forward Victory, phase I (October 1955 - March 1956) and Operation Victory, phase II (March 1956 – March 1957). The following are major events that resulted from the campaign. For more: click here.

Operation Forward Victory, phase I
Operation “Forward to Victory” 18 November. The Battle of Flags escalated during Autumn of 1955 (September – November) and peaked during January and February 1956. Schoolboys were participating in riots by throwing stones at police and some of them were trained to carry out assassinations and throw explosives. By the end of Febuary 1956: British forces were supressing a school children revolt that left one boy shot dead and the island’s school system almost completely closed.

Operation Victory, phase II
The British government exiled Makarios III (The Greek-Cypriot President at the time) to Mahe island in Seychelles on 9th March 1956 due to the failure Makarios-Harding negotiations. This triggered a week-long series of 246 attacks, including the attempted assassinations of John Harding that ended on 31st March 1956.
At around this period: EOKA totalled 53 riots, 104 house bombings, 403 ambushes, 35 attacks on police, 38 attacks on soldiers, 43 raids on police stations and 136 acts of Sabotage.

Over 30,000 British troops were assigned to combat EOKA and 104 of which were killed. EOKA killed 84 Turkish-Cypriots in the village of Tochni, leaving 1 survivor. 126 were massacred in the villages of Maratha, Santalaris and Aloda. 7 British soldiers were killed and 21 were burned to death by accident.

10th May 1956: the first 2 EOKA prisoners were hanged. The EOKA leader responded by executing 2 British soldiers. On 9th August 1956: Britain hanged 3 more prisoners.





Turkish Invasion

Background
Turkish invasion of Cyprus: July 20, 1974- August 18, 1974

Turkey justified the invasion with a provision in article IV of the Treaty of Guarantees. The provision reserves the right of the guarantor to take action and re-establish Cyprus’ current state of affairs.

The invasion saw 2 operations from the Turkish side: Attila 1 (20 July- 23 July) and Attila 2 (14 August - 16 August).

Turkey committed M47s, M48s, North American F-100 Super Sabres, Patton Tanks, Starfighter Aircrafts and Lockheed F-104s to the invasion. 40,000 troops were deployed.

Cyprus’ available forces were the Cypriot National Guard, paramilitary and militias groups (suck as EOKA-B). The military was well-trained but ill-equipped, with armoured force comprising of poorly-maintained Russian T-34/58 tanks from WWII (operated by the Cypriot National Guard). They also lacked an air force and navy and could deploy approximately 14,000 active-duty troops. Makarios III fled his office in Nicosia in morning of 15th July as The Cypriot National Guard, under command of Greek officers, burned down presidential palace.

First Engagment
On 20th – 23rd July. Turkish forces established a beachhead along the Northern Coast and then drove a narrow corridor to Nicosia (the capital).

On 20th July at early hours, 3,000 Turkish troops arrived at Pentemili beach. The landing forces consisted of 1 battalion of the 6th Amphibious Infantry Regiment, the 50th Infantry regiment, 1 company of the 39th divisional tank battalion and the Cakmak Special Strike Force Landing Brigade. 2 Greek-Cypriot motor torpedo boats (a T-3 and T-1) were dispatched from the Kyrenia harbour to engage in the enemy flotilla at around 05:00 AM. The T-1 sank by a 40mm anti-aircraft fire. The T-3 was taken down by combined fire from aircrafts and ships.

The first Greek-Cypriot units to reach Pentemili were 2 companies of the 251st infantry battalion, supported by 5 T-34 tanks and the 2nd Company of 23ΕΜΑ Medium Tank battalion. They would engage at around 10:00 AM local time. Turkish forces continued 1 km westward and proceeded further east following the 251st battalion retreatment. Many battles continued throughout the invasion. To see all operations during the invasion: click here

Aftermath
For the Turkish: the invasion tolled approximately 1500-3500 Turkish military personnel and civilian deaths, consisting of 270 civilians killed and 803 civilians missing, along with 2,000 wounded.

For Greece and Cyprus: the invasion tolled approximately 4,500-6,000 military personnel and civilian casualties, including 309 Cypriot and 105 Greek military deaths, 1,000 – 1,100 missing (as of 2015) and 12,000 wounded.

The UN (United Nations) brokered a cease-fire deal that came into effect on 16th August 1974 at 6:00 PM. A demilitarised zone that was initially established in the last days of 1963 was extended in 1974 after the cease fire. The buffer zone (known as the Green Line or Attila line) was extended following the invasion and is still in affect to this day. 20th July 1974: The United Nation Security Council Resolution 353 was unanimously adopted, demanding the withdrawal of all foreign military forces present on the island.

15th November, 1983. Turkey declares the invaded north of Cyprus as an independent country. Officially known as the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (TRNC) or unofficially as the Turkish-Cypriot territory. The country is 3,354km², taking up approximately 36% of the island.





Is Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus a Real Country?

The only countries that recognised the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus shortly after its independence were Bangladesh, Pakistan and Turkey. However: the countries (excluding Turkey) withdrew their recognition following the UNSC (United Nation Security Council) Resolution 541 on 18th November 1983.

This is because the country was self-declared and the island was unofficially divided.

Currently: the only country that recognises the TRNC is Turkey
